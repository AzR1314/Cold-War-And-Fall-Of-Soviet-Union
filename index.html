Cold War Timeline: 1970–1991

Explore the Decline of the Soviet Union and the End of the Cold War

1970–1980: Soviet Economy and Afghanistan

The Soviet economy stagnated in the 1970s due to central planning inefficiencies and heavy reliance on oil exports. The Soviet-Afghan War (1979–1989) further strained resources and morale, accelerating decline.

1969–1980: U.S. Policy of Détente

The U.S. pursued détente to ease tensions with the USSR, marked by SALT I (1972) and Nixon’s Moscow visit. This period of cooperation ended with Reagan’s confrontational approach in the 1980s.

Détente reduced the risk of nuclear war but didn’t halt Soviet internal struggles.

1985–1991: Gorbachev’s Reforms

Mikhail Gorbachev introduced perestroika (restructuring) and glasnost (openness) to revive the economy and liberalize society, but these reforms destabilized the Soviet system.

1989–1991: Velvet Revolutions & Nationalism

Eastern Europe saw peaceful “Velvet Revolutions” (e.g., Poland’s Solidarity, Czechoslovakia), while nationalist movements in Soviet republics like the Baltics eroded central control.

The rise of nationalism fueled independence demands across the USSR.

1989–1991: Berlin Wall & USSR Collapse

The Berlin Wall fell on November 9, 1989, signaling the Cold War’s end. The USSR officially dissolved on December 26, 1991, ending the superpower era.
